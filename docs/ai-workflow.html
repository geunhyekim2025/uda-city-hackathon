It was a loop, not a one-shot

The value came from iterating: the human asked domain questions, the AI ran SUEWS and analysed, the AI checked its own result, and the human scrutinised every figure and pushed for honesty.

Several findings exist only because the human asked the right question and the AI then interrogated its own answer. "Is a dry-bulb threshold appropriate for a humid city?" led to the wet-bulb re-scoring (and the discovery that a naïve threshold saturates). "Why cool roofs and greening?" led to the lever-by-lever decomposition (and the counter-intuitive finding that greening alone slightly warms the air). "Is this really the best way to show it?" led to the heat-shaded risk table and the framework diagram. The AI was treated as a fast, tireless analyst that must justify every step — not an oracle.

Prompts that mattered

"Read risk_bridge.md and risk_bridge.py in full, plus the lead example prompt. Summarise what the bridge computes, what SUEWS outputs it needs, and where the docs flag limitations. Don't run anything yet." — comprehension before action.
"Which is hottest, which is highest-risk, and are they the same? Explain the divergence in terms of urban form — roughness, mixing, exposure, vulnerability."
"The docs flag that 35 °C dry-bulb is inappropriate for a humid city. Re-compute the hazard using a humid-heat metric, and show how the ranking changes." — surfaced the saturation trap.
"Run a cool-roof + greening intervention; quantify the reduction versus baseline — and is a comparison table of different interventions useful here?" — led to the lever decomposition.
"Is the ranking robust, or an artefact of one threshold/weighting?" — led to the 34/35/36 °C and leave-one-out sweep.

Where AI + human cross-checking changed the answer

Each of these is a place a single-pass answer would have been wrong, misleading, or unverified.

✓ Caught a saturating metric

The first humid pass kept the 35 °C threshold on apparent temperature. A self-check found AT runs ~6 °C hotter than air temperature, so every neighbourhood read "dangerous" ~800 h — the signal collapsed. Fix: threshold each metric at its own danger level. Lesson: move the metric and threshold together.

✓ Exposed a scoring artefact

After the intervention cut Kampong Lama's danger 60%, its relative index stayed at 1.00 and untreated areas' indices rose without getting hotter. Traced to min–max scaling → became a headline caveat, and motivated a new absolute metric (person-hours).

✓ Investigated a counter-intuitive result

The decomposition showed greening alone slightly raised air temperature. Rather than hide it, the AI dug into why: the dataset's evergreen trees have albedo 0.14 — darker than the 0.20 pavement they replace — and evaporation is water-limited. Reported as an honest finding.

✓ Stress-tested its own ranking

Re-ran the risk ranking at 34/35/36 °C and with each vulnerability proxy removed in turn — identical every time. The headline is structural, not a product of one arbitrary choice.

✓ Quantified instead of asserting

"Smoother places are hotter" was not left as a claim — the AI regressed dangerous hours on roughness (frontal-area density) and reported R² = 0.73.

✓ Used physically-consistent inputs

Used the model's own 2 m humidity (RH2/Q2, height-consistent with the modelled temperature) for the wet-bulb metric, rather than splicing forcing-level RH — and stated the choice so a reviewer can object.

Innovation — analyses we built beyond the brief

  • Person-hours & vulnerable person-hours — an absolute, transferable hazard×exposure metric that fixes the relative index's blind spot.
  • Time-resolved (day vs night) risk — pairing the humid-heat hazard with the matching daytime/nighttime population (the dataset's unused population_night).
  • Lever-by-lever intervention decomposition + the evidence-led optimum (cool roofs + cool pavements), and a greening dose under +2.5 °C.
  • Heatwave-event metrics (longest spell, multi-day events, degree-hours) and the roughness→heat regression.
  • A SUEWS assess_readiness provenance check — confirming zero assumed sample defaults before trusting the config.

Guardrails — how the AI was kept honest

  • Validate before every long run. suews validate (zero errors) on each edited config before simulating — catching structure issues up front, not after a narrative was written.
  • SUEWS MCP tools as checks — validate_config and assess_readiness used to confirm the config is valid and genuinely site-specific.
  • Reproducibility by construction — every figure and table comes from a script; the whole analysis reruns with one command and returns the same numbers.
  • State choices for objection — thresholds, weights and assumptions are surfaced (and the anthropogenic-heat term is flagged as switched off), so a reviewer can disagree on the record.

Engineering issues the agent caught and fixed

  • matplotlib.cm.get_cmap removed in the installed version → switched to plt.get_cmap.
  • GitHub Pages "build failed" because it ran Jekyll on hand-written static HTML → added docs/.nojekyll.
  • A legend bug (empty-bar handles rendered every swatch blue) → rebuilt with proper colour patches; label overlaps on three figures → leader-line placement.
  • Verified the live site end-to-end after every push (asset returns 200, content served) rather than assuming deployment.

Contribution to the SUEWS community

Reusable findings drafted for the SUEWS Discourse hackathon channel — best practice and a critical suggestion, drawn from the work above.

Best practice

A humid-heat threshold has to move with the metric

Switching air temperature → apparent temperature but keeping a 35 °C threshold saturates the hazard (~800 h "dangerous" everywhere) and scrambles the ranking. Pair every metric with a metric-appropriate threshold (e.g. wet-bulb > 28 °C). Swapping the metric alone is a silent failure mode.

Critical suggestion

Relative (min–max) risk indices hide interventions

A min–max index can show a 60%-effective intervention as helping nobody (the worst site stays at 1.0) and can inflate untreated sites. Report the absolute hazard change (hours of dangerous heat avoided) alongside any relative index, and note such indices do not transfer between cities or states.

Best practice

Validate before you run; use the model's own 2 m state

Run suews validate before every long simulation, and use SUEWS' own RH2/Q2 for humid-heat indices rather than splicing forcing-level RH onto the modelled temperature.
